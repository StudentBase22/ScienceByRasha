Module 1: Fundamentals of Research

1) What is Research?

Research is a systematic investigation process used to discover new knowledge, solve problems, answer questions, and understand phenomena.

Definitions:
  • Kerlinger: Research is a systematic, controlled, empirical, and critical investigation of natural phenomena.
  • Babbie: Scientific inquiry is how scientists ask questions, investigate, gather evidence, and propose explanations.
  • Basley & Clover: Research is obtaining accurate answers to important questions using scientific methods.

Features: Scientific, Systematic, Controlled, Empirical, Critical, Problem-solving oriented, Knowledge-building.

2) Research and Knowledge

Knowledge is information collected by humans over time. Information becomes knowledge when it is Organized, Structured, Given meaning, and Used for understanding.

Important: Every science is knowledge, but not every knowledge is science.

3) & 4) Purposes of Research

General purposes: Get new facts, understand behavior, answer questions, solve problems, evaluate alternatives, predict outcomes.

Exploration

Used when info is limited. Explores ideas, builds foundation.

Description

Answers "What?". Identifies patterns, records characteristics.

Explanation

Answers "Why?". The central goal! Finds cause-effect relationships.

Prediction

Answers "What will happen?". Forecasts outcomes.

5) & 6) Why Conduct Research & Importance

Get Degrees Solve Problems Intellectual Enjoyment Service to Society Business Planning Technology Development

Module 2: Scientific Concepts & Reasoning

7) Important Scientific Concepts

Concept Meaning Detail
Fact Proven observation Confirmed by repeated observation
Hypothesis Testable assumption Starting point of investigation
Theory Explains why Verified explanation of natural phenomenon
Law Predicts what Statement that summarizes observations

8) & 9) Comparisons

Prediction vs Explanation
  • Prediction: Future, forecasts results. (e.g. Sales will increase)
  • Explanation: Why or how, focuses on reasons. (e.g. Sales increased because of marketing)
Hypothesis vs Prediction
  • Hypothesis: Testable explanation. (e.g. Study more = score better)
  • Prediction: Expected result. (e.g. Studying 5 hours = higher marks)

10) Inductive vs Deductive Reasoning

Inductive

Specific General

Observations → General Rules → Predictions
Deductive

General Specific

Theory → Applied to cases → Tests hypotheses

Module 4: The Research Problem

14) What is it?

A specific issue, gap, or unanswered question that research tries to solve. Shows the difference between known and unknown.

15) Types of Research Problems

Practical Problems

Goal: Create change

Examples: High turnover, poor performance

Found via: Workplace discussions, reports

Theoretical Problems

Goal: Expand knowledge

Examples: Income inequality, unknown relationships

Found via: Literature, theories, gaps

Module 6: Formulating a Research Problem (Part 2)

21) Research Process Overview

Research follows structured steps:

  • Select research problem
  • Formulate problem statement
  • Select research design
  • Collect data
  • Analyze & interpret data
  • Report writing

22) What is a Research Problem?

It is the starting point of research. Defines what you will study and why. A topic alone is not enough—you need a clear, focused problem.

A research problem statement is: Brief, Precise, Focused, Describes the issue to be investigated.

23) Purpose of a Research Problem Statement

  • Place the problem in context
  • Explain importance
  • Identify knowledge gaps
  • Show implications of the study
  • Guide research questions and hypotheses

24) & 25) Key Elements & Importance

Key Elements
  • Contextualization (background)
  • Clear description
  • Significance
  • Scope (boundaries)
  • Potential impact
Importance
  • Gives focus and direction
  • Clarifies research purpose
  • Justifies why research is needed
  • Foundation for questions, objectives, methodology

26) & 27) Steps & Components

Steps: State problem clearly, Identify variables, Define variables operationally, Develop hypotheses, Form research questions, Evaluate feasibility.

Components:

Purpose of study Significance Research questions Hypotheses Operational definitions Scope & limitations

28) & 29) Purpose Statement & Research Significance

Purpose Statement: Explains what the study will do. Must specify: Topic, Population, Setting. Can focus on: Exploration, Relationships, Effects.

Significance (Justification): Explains why the study matters and what contribution it makes. Based on: Gaps in previous research, Importance of issue, Theoretical support.

30) & 31) Research Questions & Characteristics

Central questions guiding research. Types:

Descriptive

What is happening?

Relational

Relationship between variables

Causal

Cause-effect relationships

Characteristics of Good Questions: Clear and specific, Feasible, Relevant, Balanced (not too broad/narrow), Testable/arguable. (Not yes/no).

32) Hypotheses

Testable predictions based on theory and prior knowledge, not random guesses. Must be measurable and testable.

Example: "Sun exposure increases happiness"

33) Variables

Variables = measurable characteristics.

  • Independent variable → cause
  • Dependent variable → effect

Operational Definition: Defines how a variable is measured. Ensures Clarity, Consistency, Replicability.

34) & 35) Limitations & Literature Review Role

Limitations: Must be acknowledged (Time constraints, Financial limits, Early-stage research). Avoid exaggerating research value.

Literature Review Role: Identify what is known, Find research gaps, Refine problem, Understand theoretical approaches.

36), 37) & 38) Research Proposal

Definition: A detailed plan of research including what, why, how, and expected results.

Purpose: Convince others that the idea is valuable, you understand the field, and you can complete the study.

Components: Title & abstract, Introduction, Problem statement, Literature review, Methodology, Project plan, Conclusion, References.

Chapter 2 → Literature review

Module 8: Data Collection and Sampling Techniques (Part 2)

1. Sample and Sampling Frame

A sample is a smaller but representative collection of units taken from a population to determine truths about that population.

Sampling is used because:

  • It saves time
  • It reduces costs
  • It reduces workload
  • It provides results with known mathematical accuracy

A sampling frame is the list from which respondents are selected.

Examples: Registrar's office lists, Class rosters

2. Population of Interest

Before sampling, researchers must identify the population to which they want to generalize their findings.

Examples: All doctors, School children, Indians, Women aged 15–45 years

3. Population Structure

Research populations are divided into:

  • Target (Theoretical) Population
  • Study Population
  • Sample

4. Sampling Breakdown

Researchers should ask: Can the entire population be sampled?

Sampling the whole population may be possible when:

  • Population size is very small
  • Resources are extensive
  • A low response rate is expected

5. Factors Affecting Sample Representativeness

Three factors influence representativeness:

  • Sampling procedure
  • Sample size
  • Participation (response rate)

6. Sampling Process

The sampling process includes:

  • Defining the population
  • Specifying a sampling frame
  • Selecting a sampling method
  • Determining sample size
  • Implementing the sampling plan and collecting data
  • Reviewing the process and assessing response rate

Probability Sampling

Definition: A probability sample is one where every population unit has a chance greater than zero of being selected and the probability can be accurately calculated.

Equal Probability of Selection (EPS): Every population element has the same selection probability. Also called self-weighting designs because every unit receives the same weight.

Types of Probability Sampling:

  • Simple Random Sampling
  • Systematic Sampling
  • Stratified Random Sampling
  • Cluster Sampling

Simple Random Sampling

Characteristics:

  • Each element has an equal and independent chance of selection
  • Each unit receives a number
  • Random number tables or lotteries are used

Suitable when: Population is small, Population is homogeneous, Population is easily available

Types:

  • Without Replacement (WOR) - A unit cannot be selected twice
  • With Replacement (WR) - A unit may appear multiple times

Systematic Sampling

Steps:

  • Arrange population according to an ordering scheme
  • Select members at regular intervals

Formula: k = Population Size / Sample Size

Procedure: Start randomly, Select every kth element afterward

Example: Every 10th name in a telephone directory

Stratified Random Sampling

Used when population contains different categories.

Process:

  • Divide population into strata
  • Sample each stratum separately

Characteristics: Equal selection chance within each stratum, Same sampling fraction ensures proportional representation

Cluster Sampling

A two-stage sampling method.

Stage 1: Select clusters (areas)

Stage 2: Select respondents within chosen clusters

Characteristics: Population divided into homogeneous clusters, Usually based on geographical location, Entire selected clusters are studied

Non-Probability Sampling

Definition: Occurs when some population members have no chance of selection, Selection probability cannot be determined, Selection depends on assumptions about the population.

Example: Interviewing the first person answering the door in every house.

Types:

  • Quota Sampling
  • Convenience Sampling
  • Judgmental Sampling
  • Snowball Sampling

Quota Sampling

Steps: Divide population into mutually exclusive groups, Select participants based on judgment and specified proportions.

Characteristics: Non-random selection

Example: 200 females and 300 males aged 45–60

Convenience Sampling

Characteristics: Uses participants who are readily available, Easy and inexpensive, Not representative of the population, Commonly used in pilot studies.

Example: Surveying shoppers present early in the morning

Judgmental Sampling

Characteristics: Researcher selects participants believed to be appropriate, Used when expertise is required.

Example: Studying luxury car buyers by selecting likely luxury car owners

Snowball Sampling

Characteristics: Participants recruit additional participants, Useful for hard-to-find populations.

Example: Individuals with rare diseases

Data Analysis Process

Researchers should plan analysis early.

Steps:

  • Code data
  • Enter data
  • Clean data

  • Interpret data

Research Analysis Plan

A research analysis plan is a blueprint explaining how data will be organized, analyzed, and interpreted.

Components:

  • Research problem
  • Research purpose
  • Research questions
  • Research variables
  • Analysis techniques

Data Coding

Definition: The process of assigning labels or codes to organize and classify data.

Purpose: Identify themes, Detect patterns, Find relationships

Types of Coding:

  • Nominal Coding: Assigns labels/categories (e.g., Single=1, Married=2, Divorced=3, Widowed=4)
  • Ordinal Coding: Assigns ordered categories (e.g., Very dissatisfied=1, Frustrated=2, Neutral=3, Satisfied=4, Very satisfied=5)
  • Numeric Coding: Assigns numerical values (e.g., Age 18–24=1, Age 25–34=2, Age 35–44=3)
  • Dichotomous Coding: Uses binary values (e.g., Male=0, Female=1)

Data Cleaning

Purpose: Remove bad data, Improve reliability

Steps:

  • Remove white spaces and formatting errors
  • Remove duplicates and unwanted data
  • Structure the data

Reflecting on Findings

Researchers should ask:

  • What did we learn?
  • What conclusions can be drawn?
  • What recommendations should be made?
  • What are the limitations?

Data Presentation

Tabular Representation: Used when data are too detailed for text. Benefits: Highlights patterns, Improves readability.

Graphical Representation: Visual presentation of data and relationships.

Rules: Suitable title, Measurement unit, Proper scale, Legend (index), Simplicity.

Types of Graphs:

  • Line Graph: Displays continuous data, Useful for future predictions
  • Bar Graph: Compares categories using bars
  • Histogram: Shows frequency distributions, Equal-width continuous intervals
  • Line Plot: Shows frequency on a number line, Uses X marks
  • Circle Graph (Pie Chart): Shows parts of a whole, Total circle = 100%

Software for Analysis

Quantitative Analysis: SPSS, SAS

Qualitative Analysis: Epi Info, In Vivo

General Tools: Excel, Microsoft Access

Software helps: Search data, Organize data, Categorize data, Annotate data, Visualize relationships

Module 10: Data Analysis and Interpretation (Part 2)

Quantitative Research

Characteristics: Measures variables numerically, Focuses on numerical summaries, Examines relationships among phenomena.

Objectives: Develop mathematical models, theories, hypotheses.

Measurement is essential because it connects observations and data.

Qualitative Research

Focuses on: Experiences, Perceptions, Behaviors.

Answers: How? Why?

Rather than: How many? How much?

May include descriptive statistics.

Quantitative Analysis Techniques

1. Clustering Analysis: Grouping similar data points into clusters. Goals: Homogeneous within clusters, Heterogeneous between clusters. Applications: Customer segmentation. Examples: Hierarchical clustering, Expectation Maximization, K-medians, K-means.

2. Regression Analysis: Statistical technique for estimating relationships between variables. Uses: Measure relationship strength, Build prediction models, Forecast future outcomes.

Regression Equation: Y = a + bX + ε

Where: Y = Dependent variable, X = Independent variable, a = Intercept, b = Slope, ε = Error term

Regression Line: A straight line showing the best-fit relationship between dependent and independent variables.

3. Monte Carlo Analysis: Provides statistical predictions by varying model parameters randomly within specified limits. Generated values follow a statistical distribution.

4. Time Series Analysis: Analyzes data collected at consistent intervals over time. Purpose: Detect trends and patterns over time.

5. Cohort Analysis: Behavioral analytics method. Characteristics: Divides data into cohorts, Cohorts share characteristics or experiences within a time period.

Qualitative Analysis Techniques

1. Content Analysis: Examines words, themes, concepts. Used to identify meanings, relationships, frequencies.

2. Sentiment Analysis: Uses Machine Learning, Statistics, Natural Language Processing (NLP). Purpose: Determine positive or negative sentiment.

3. Thematic Analysis: Process: Read qualitative data, Identify recurring themes and patterns. Researcher's interpretation is central.

4. Discourse Analysis: Studies how context affects language meaning. Example: Meaning of "home" changes depending on who is being addressed.

5. Grounded Theory: Develops theories directly from data. Uses interviews, observations. Creates theory inductively rather than testing existing theories.

Four Categories of Data Analysis

1. Descriptive Analysis: Answers: What happened? Purpose: Summarize data, Describe characteristics.

2. Diagnostic Analysis: Answers: Why did it happen? Purpose: Identify underlying causes.

3. Predictive Analysis: Answers: What is likely to happen? Uses: Historical data, Machine Learning, Artificial Intelligence.

4. Prescriptive Analysis: Answers: What should be done? Purpose: Recommend the best course of action.

  • Formulating the problem statement

  • Collecting data
  • Analyzing and interpreting data
  • Report writing

The research process also includes:

  • Preparing the research setting
  • Selecting the sample
  • Collecting data
  • Analyzing data
  • Interpreting findings
  • Reporting results

2. Importance of Statistics in Research

Statistics are essential because they provide tools to collect, analyze, interpret data, draw meaningful conclusions, and make informed decisions.

Statistical methods help researchers validate research methodologies, ensure reliability and validity, test hypotheses, and validate scientific theories.

Roles of Statistics: Selecting subjects for study, Assigning subjects to groups, Describing collected data, Drawing inferences and generalizing findings to larger populations.

3. Types of Statistics

A. Descriptive Statistics: Methods used to characterize and summarize collected data. Functions: Organize data, Summarize data, Simplify data, Present data clearly. Purpose: Describe data.

B. Inferential Statistics: Methods used to study relationships among variables, test hypotheses, generalize findings from samples to populations, make predictions.

4. Common Descriptive Statistical Measures

  • Frequency (Count)
  • Percentage
  • Mean
  • Mode
  • Median
  • Range
  • Variance
  • Standard Deviation
  • Ranking (replacing values with positions in sorted order)

5. Characteristics of Descriptive Statistics

Descriptive statistics characterize collected data, focus on the sample being studied, describe individuals, describe collections of individuals, and serve as the foundation for inferential statistics.

6. Choosing Measures of Central Tendency and Variability

Mean: Use when data are equal interval data.

Median: Use when data are ordinal or data are highly skewed interval data.

Interquartile Range: Use when median is selected as the average.

Range: Use sparingly as a measure of variability.

Standard Deviation: Use when mean is selected as the average. Important: Standard deviation has a special relationship with the normal distribution curve.

7. Three Types of Descriptive Statistics

1. Frequency Distributions: Show number of subjects in each category. Cross-Tabulation categorizes data based on more than one variable simultaneously.

2. Graphical Representations: Examples: Polygon graphs, Line graphs.

3. Summary Statistics: Describe data using a single numerical value.

8. Inferential Statistics

Inferential statistics generalize characteristics from sample data to larger populations, describe populations using samples, test differences between two samples, between sample and population, or between two measures of the same population.

9. Types of Data

Categorical Variables:

  • Nominal Variables: No intrinsic ordering (e.g., Gender Male/Female)
  • Ordinal Variables: Have a meaningful ranking (e.g., Survey scale from "Strongly Disagree" to "Strongly Agree"). Distances between categories are not necessarily equal.

Numerical Variables:

  • Interval Data: Equal distances between values, No absolute zero (e.g., Temperature, Year)
  • Ratio Data: Has an absolute zero point (e.g., Distance, Area)

10. Statistical Tests and Models

Numeric vs Numeric Data: Example: Body fat vs skinfold measurements. Model: Linear Regression. Purpose: Predict Y using X, Describe how response variable changes with explanatory variable.

Numeric vs Nominal Data: Example: Strength vs Sex. t-Test: Used when comparing two groups. Purpose: Determine whether a statistically significant difference exists between means.

One-Way ANOVA: Used when comparing more than two groups. Purpose: Determine whether population means differ significantly.

Nominal vs Nominal Data: Example: Sport vs Sex. Chi-Square Test: Used to compare observed and expected results, determine whether a relationship exists between categorical variables, determine whether differences occur by chance or due to actual relationships.

11. Multivariate Statistics

Multivariate statistics analyze more than two variables simultaneously. Can be descriptive or inferential.

Dependence Techniques: Study cause-and-effect relationships. Examples: Multiple Regression, Discriminant Analysis, ANOVA, MANOVA.

Interdependence Techniques: No variable is classified as dependent or independent. All variables are analyzed together. Examples: Factor Analysis, Cluster Analysis. Purpose: Explore dataset structure.

Module 12: Research Project Reporting

1. Research Project Definition

A research project is a systematic investigation of a specific topic, designed to answer research questions or objectives.

It includes: Literature review, Research design, Data collection, Data analysis, Reporting findings.

Characteristics: Structured, Complex, Often iterative, Requires planning, execution, and communication.

2. Important Considerations

Time Management: Allocate enough time for each stage.

Resource Management: Consider: Time, Funding, Expertise.

Ethical Considerations: Research must be conducted ethically.

Supervision: Seek guidance from supervisors or mentors.

3. Types of Research Projects

Academic Research: Theses, Dissertations.

Market Research: Consumer behavior studies, Market trend studies.

Scientific Research: Experiments, Scientific investigations.

Social Science Research: Surveys, Qualitative studies of society and behavior.

4. Types of Research Reports

Dissertations/Theses: Prepared for MSc and PhD degrees.

Journal Papers: Prepared for publication.

Independent Reports: Prepared for organizations to solve specific problems.

5. Academic vs Consultancy Reports

Academic Report: Longer, Graded, Contains contextual descriptions.

Consultancy Report: Less theoretical, Focuses on business recommendations.

6. Purpose of Research Reports

Research reports present results and persuade readers of findings.

Purposes:

  • Informative: Describe current situations
  • Explanatory: Explain situations and suggest actions
  • Persuasive: Investigate problems and recommend actions

7. Characteristics of a Good Report

A report should perform a clear function, present information logically, communicate ideas clearly, use critically analyzed evidence, offer recommendations, be accurately referenced, and be error-free.

8. Proposal vs Research Report

Both have similar structures.

Proposal: Includes project plan.

Report: Includes results and discussion of findings.

Proposal sections may later be copied into the final report after modifications such as changing future tense into past tense.

9. Research Report Structure

Title Page: Includes research title, researcher's name, institution, sponsoring organization, supervisor/co-supervisor, date. A good title shows focus, shows boundaries, is concise, is specific, may indicate independent and dependent variables.

Abstract: Approximately 300 words. Should answer: Research questions, Hypotheses, Methodology, Findings.

Introduction: Purpose: Provide context, Explain importance of research. Includes: Theoretical background, Background information on the problem.

Problem Statement: Should include: Research idea/problem, Research motivation, Justification, Importance, Research questions/objectives, Hypotheses, Independent and dependent variables, Research boundaries, Constraints, Operational definitions.

Literature Review: Goals: Identify major findings, Identify deficiencies and gaps, Lead toward research questions. Functions: Provide theoretical foundation, Identify knowledge gaps, Demonstrate understanding, Give credit to previous researchers. It is NOT a simple list of sources or a collection of summaries.

Methodology: Includes: Research design/model, Participants, Sampling, Instruments, Validity and reliability, Data analysis techniques. Methodology should explain philosophical stance, describe methods, explain rationale, provide enough information for evaluating soundness.

Results and Discussion:

  • Results Section: Presents findings objectively, Uses tables, figures, and statistics, Avoids interpretation.
  • Discussion Section: Interprets findings, Explains significance, Relates findings to theory and previous research, Discusses limitations.

Difference: Results = What was found. Discussion = What findings mean.

Conclusion: Functions: Summarize findings, Highlight significance, Discuss implications, Acknowledge limitations, Suggest future research, Leave a lasting impression. Steps: Restate problem, Summarize findings, Discuss implications, Mention limitations, Suggest future research, End with a strong statement.

References: Use accepted referencing styles, Cite all sources, Prevent plagiarism.

APA Citation Style: Parenthetical: (Field, 2005), (Park et al., 1994). Narrative: Grady et al. (2019).

Appendices: Should include only essential supporting materials, Include interview schedules when necessary.

Writing and Presentation Tips

  • Choose an appropriate title
  • Tell a clear and truthful story
  • Use a clear structure
  • Summarize chapters
  • Use tables and graphics
  • Write clearly and simply
  • Avoid jargon
  • Check grammar and spelling
  • Preserve anonymity
  • Revise drafts regularly

Additional advice: Writing clarifies thinking, Information should be accessible, Rewrite drafts multiple times, Include all important literature, Prepare presentations carefully, Use visual aids, Tell them what you're going to say, say it, then tell them what you said.
