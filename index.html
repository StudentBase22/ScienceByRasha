Module 1: Fundamentals of Research

1) What is Research?

Research is a systematic investigation process used to discover new knowledge, solve problems, answer questions, and understand phenomena.

Definitions:
  • Kerlinger: Research is a systematic, controlled, empirical, and critical investigation of natural phenomena.
  • Babbie: Scientific inquiry is how scientists ask questions, investigate, gather evidence, and propose explanations.
  • Basley & Clover: Research is obtaining accurate answers to important questions using scientific methods.

Features: Scientific, Systematic, Controlled, Empirical, Critical, Problem-solving oriented, Knowledge-building.

2) Research and Knowledge

Knowledge is information collected by humans over time. Information becomes knowledge when it is Organized, Structured, Given meaning, and Used for understanding.

Important: Every science is knowledge, but not every knowledge is science.

3) & 4) Purposes of Research

General purposes: Get new facts, understand behavior, answer questions, solve problems, evaluate alternatives, predict outcomes.

Exploration

Used when info is limited. Explores ideas, builds foundation.

Description

Answers "What?". Identifies patterns, records characteristics.

Explanation

Answers "Why?". The central goal! Finds cause-effect relationships.

Prediction

Answers "What will happen?". Forecasts outcomes.

5) & 6) Why Conduct Research & Importance

Get Degrees Solve Problems Intellectual Enjoyment Service to Society Business Planning Technology Development

Module 2: Scientific Concepts & Reasoning

7) Important Scientific Concepts

Concept Meaning Detail
Fact Proven observation Confirmed by repeated observation
Hypothesis Testable assumption Starting point of investigation
Theory Explains why Verified explanation of natural phenomenon
Law Predicts what Statement that summarizes observations

8) & 9) Comparisons

Prediction vs Explanation
  • Prediction: Future, forecasts results. (e.g. Sales will increase)
  • Explanation: Why or how, focuses on reasons. (e.g. Sales increased because of marketing)
Hypothesis vs Prediction
  • Hypothesis: Testable explanation. (e.g. Study more = score better)
  • Prediction: Expected result. (e.g. Studying 5 hours = higher marks)

10) Inductive vs Deductive Reasoning

Inductive

Specific General

Observations → General Rules → Predictions
Deductive

General Specific

Theory → Applied to cases → Tests hypotheses

Module 4: The Research Problem

14) What is it?

A specific issue, gap, or unanswered question that research tries to solve. Shows the difference between known and unknown.

15) Types of Research Problems

Practical Problems

Goal: Create change

Examples: High turnover, poor performance

Found via: Workplace discussions, reports

Theoretical Problems

Goal: Expand knowledge

Examples: Income inequality, unknown relationships

Found via: Literature, theories, gaps

Module 5: Topic Selection

16) & 19) Choosing a Topic

Base it on: Interest, Knowledge, Skills, Feasibility, Available Data, Time, Contribution.

Ask Yourself: Am I interested? Can I do it? Is data/literature available? Do I have time?

17) & 18) Steps & Sources

Sources: Observation, Discussion, Literature, Workplace, Brainstorming.

  • Be curious & observe
  • Read & talk to colleagues

  • Narrow the topic

20) Narrowing Topic Example

Module 6: Formulating a Research Problem (Part 2)

21) Research Process Overview

Research follows structured steps:

  • Select research problem
  • Formulate problem statement
  • Select research design
  • Collect data
  • Analyze & interpret data
  • Report writing

22) What is a Research Problem?

It is the starting point of research. Defines what you will study and why. A topic alone is not enough—you need a clear, focused problem.

A research problem statement is: Brief, Precise, Focused, Describes the issue to be investigated.

23) Purpose of a Research Problem Statement

  • Place the problem in context
  • Explain importance
  • Identify knowledge gaps
  • Show implications of the study
  • Guide research questions and hypotheses

24) & 25) Key Elements & Importance

Key Elements
  • Contextualization (background)
  • Clear description
  • Significance
  • Scope (boundaries)
  • Potential impact
Importance
  • Gives focus and direction
  • Clarifies research purpose
  • Justifies why research is needed
  • Foundation for questions, objectives, methodology

26) & 27) Steps & Components

Steps: State problem clearly, Identify variables, Define variables operationally, Develop hypotheses, Form research questions, Evaluate feasibility.

Components:

Purpose of study Significance Research questions Hypotheses Operational definitions Scope & limitations

28) & 29) Purpose Statement & Research Significance

Purpose Statement: Explains what the study will do. Must specify: Topic, Population, Setting. Can focus on: Exploration, Relationships, Effects.

Significance (Justification): Explains why the study matters and what contribution it makes. Based on: Gaps in previous research, Importance of issue, Theoretical support.

30) & 31) Research Questions & Characteristics

Central questions guiding research. Types:

Descriptive

What is happening?

Relational

Relationship between variables

Causal

Cause-effect relationships

Characteristics of Good Questions: Clear and specific, Feasible, Relevant, Balanced (not too broad/narrow), Testable/arguable. (Not yes/no).

32) Hypotheses

Testable predictions based on theory and prior knowledge, not random guesses. Must be measurable and testable.

Example: "Sun exposure increases happiness"

33) Variables

Variables = measurable characteristics.

  • Independent variable → cause
  • Dependent variable → effect

Operational Definition: Defines how a variable is measured. Ensures Clarity, Consistency, Replicability.

34) & 35) Limitations & Literature Review Role

Limitations: Must be acknowledged (Time constraints, Financial limits, Early-stage research). Avoid exaggerating research value.

Literature Review Role: Identify what is known, Find research gaps, Refine problem, Understand theoretical approaches.

36), 37) & 38) Research Proposal

Definition: A detailed plan of research including what, why, how, and expected results.

Purpose: Convince others that the idea is valuable, you understand the field, and you can complete the study.

Components: Title & abstract, Introduction, Problem statement, Literature review, Methodology, Project plan, Conclusion, References.

Chapter 1 → Introduction & problem
Chapter 2 → Literature review
Chapter 3 → Methodology

Module 7: Selecting a Research Design Strategy (Part 1)

39) What is Research Design?

The overall plan/blueprint of research. Determines how data is collected and how it is analyzed.

Difference: Design = structure | Methodology = detailed procedures

40) Types of Research (Major Classifications)

Type A Type B / C Description
Basic Applied / Evaluation Basic → new knowledge; Applied → solve problems; Evaluation → assess effectiveness
Exploratory Conclusive Exploratory → early stage, flexible; Conclusive → structured, quantitative
Exploratory Descriptive / Causal Understand problem vs Describe characteristics vs Cause-effect
Conceptual Empirical Theory-based vs Data-based observation
Applied Fundamental Practical solutions vs Expand knowledge
Descriptive Analytical What is happening vs Why/how it happens
Quantitative Qualitative Numbers/statistics vs Meanings/experiences
Cross-sectional Longitudinal One point in time vs Over time
Clinical Diagnostic Study diseases/treatments vs Test accuracy of diagnosis

41) & 42) Design vs Methodology & Components

Methodology: how data is collected & analyzed. Design: overall strategy/structure. Design depends on research problem.

Methodology Components:

  • Research design model
  • Sample/participants
  • Data collection methods
  • Data analysis techniques
  • Interpretation (discussion)

43) Good Research Design Characteristics

Minimizes bias Maximizes reliability Provides max info Reduces error Covers multiple aspects

44) & 45) Main Categories & Choosing a Design

Main Categories: Exploratory, Descriptive, Causal, Experimental, Quasi-experimental, Ex post facto.

Choosing a Research Design Depends on: Research objectives, Nature of problem, Existing knowledge level.
